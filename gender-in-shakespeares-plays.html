Gender in Shakespeare's Plays

An Exploratory Data-Driven Analysis

Hundreds of Shakespearean scholars have written about gender in his plays. They have dissected each play and interpreted every word. And so it requires some temerity to speak on this subject when I haven't read all his plays. I know it is a futile exercise to analyse this topic through data derived from his plays. I'm sure I'll be losing a lot of context. But such as it is, I have focused on a simple metric: the number of words spoken by each character. By dissecting this data, I arrived at various observations—some obvious, some intriguing.

In 1852, John Herschel, son of the discoverer of Uranus, decided not to name Uranus’s moons after Greek gods. He instead chose magical spirits from English literature. Two were the fairies ‘Oberon and Titania’ from Shakespeare's "A Midsummer Night's Dream". The other two came from Alexander Pope's works. In 1949, Gerard Kuiper continued this legacy. He named the fifth moon ‘Miranda’ after the character from "The Tempest". Over the years, Margaret, Ophelia, Bianca, Cressida, Desdemona, Juliet, Portia, and Rosalind joined the orbit of the seventh planet. There's also Prospero, Oberon, Puck, and Caliban, but the majority of these distant celestial objects are Shakespeare's women. I mention this because it is one of the few places where Shakespeare's female characters outnumber the male ones.

Shakespeare wrote some great women in literature, but he wrote more about men. Men dominate Shakespeare's casts accounting for over 80% of the total characters. This trend reflects the Elizabethan times, the late 16th and early 17th centuries. A period when gender roles were defined and enforced. Men were dominant, and women subservient. Shakespeare followed these conventions but he also subverted at times. Especially when he was writing comedies.

Words spoken by characters of each gender and within each genre.

Shakespeare's women were conventional in histories. In tragedies, a little less. In comedies though, they were adventurous. They not only spoke more but with spiritedness. For instance, in "As You Like It", women speak for 40% of the play's duration, and Rosalind dominates much of the action. It is not surprising then, that among the top ten plays where women speak more, eight are comedies. Among the other two, one is a history and the other a tragedy. The historic play "Henry VI, Part II" features in the top ten because Margaret of Anjou plays a pivotal role in shaping the plot. And "Romeo and Juliet" is one of the few plays where a woman is an eponymous character.

On the other end, Timon of Athens has only 79 words spoken by women as opposed to 17,764 words spoken by men. The few plays at the bottom of the chart have similar problems. They revolve around the male-dominated political and military spheres of the time, where women had little or no place.

While women had no place in the male-dominated spheres of Elizabethan times, their characterization found a place on Shakespeare’s stage. Whether at the Globe or Blackfriars, the settings of his plays spanned from Inverness to Syria.
