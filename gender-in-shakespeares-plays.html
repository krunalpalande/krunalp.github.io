Gender in Shakespeare's Plays

An Exploratory Data-Driven Analysis

Hundreds of Shakespearean scholars have written about gender in his plays over the years, and many more will continue to do so. It requires some temerity to speak on this subject when I haven't read all of his plays. Scholars have dissected each play and interpreted every word within both Elizabethan and modern contexts. In contrast, I have undertaken a possibly futile exercise of analyzing this topic through data derived from his plays. I examined all his works, analyzed the text, and focused on a simple metric: the number of words spoken by each character. By dissecting this data, I arrived at various observations—some obvious, some intriguing. While my analysis may not yield the depth of insight a scholar could provide, I present a data-driven exploration of gender in Shakespeare's plays.

In 1852, John Herschel, son of the discoverer of Uranus, named the moons not after Greek mythology but after magical spirits from English literature. Among these names were the fairies Oberon and Titania from Shakespeare's A Midsummer Night's Dream. The other two names came from Alexander Pope's works. In 1949, Gerard Kuiper continued this Shakespearean legacy by naming the fifth moon Miranda from The Tempest. Over the years, Margaret, Ophelia, Bianca, Cressida, Desdemona, Juliet, Portia, and Rosalind joined the orbit of the seventh planet. There's also Prospero, Oberon, Puck, and Caliban, but the majority of these distant celestial objects are named after Shakespeare's women. I mention this because it is one of the few places where Shakespeare's female characters outnumber the male ones.

Shakespeare wrote some of the greatest women in literature, but he created even more great male characters. For every strong character like Lady Macbeth, Cleopatra, Juliet, Ophelia, Gertrude, Desdemona, Rosalind, and Viola, there are Macbeth, Hamlet, Othello, Iago, Falstaff, Antony, Shylock, Prospero, the Henrys, and the Richards. The list of strong male characters seems much longer. The reason for this lies in the stark difference between gender roles in his histories, tragedies, and comedies. While his women were often conventional and fewer in number in histories and tragedies, in comedies, the female roles were larger and more playful with conventions and stereotypes. Shakespeare perhaps found more freedom to write adventurous women in comedies than in other genres. Though the number of female characters and their dialogue often remained smaller compared to male characters, there is clear evidence that women spoke more in comedies and perhaps more freely.
