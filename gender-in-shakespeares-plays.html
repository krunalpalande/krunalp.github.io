Gender in Shakespeare's Plays

An Exploratory Data-Driven Analysis

Hundreds of Shakespearean scholars have written about gender in his plays. They have dissected each play and interpreted every word. It requires some temerity to speak on this subject when I haven't read all his plays. It is a futile exercise, to analyse this topic through data derived from his plays. I'm sure I'll be losing a lot of context. But such as it is. I have focused on a simple metric: the number of words spoken by each character. By dissecting this data, I arrived at various observationsâ€”some obvious, some intriguing.

In 1852, John Herschel, son of the discoverer of Uranus, decided not to name its after Greek gods. He instead chose magical spirits from English literature. Two were the fairies Oberon and Titania from Shakespeare's "A Midsummer Night's Dream". The other two came from Alexander Pope's works. In 1949, Gerard Kuiper continued this legacy. He named the fifth moon Miranda from "The Tempest", continuing the legacy. Over the years, Margaret, Ophelia, Bianca, Cressida, Desdemona, Juliet, Portia, and Rosalind joined the orbit of the seventh planet. There's also Prospero, Oberon, Puck, and Caliban, but the majority of these distant celestial objects are Shakespeare's women. I mention this because it is one of the few places where Shakespeare's female characters outnumber the male ones.

Shakespeare wrote some great women in literature. He also wrote great men, but more. Men dominate Shakespeare's casts, often accounting for over 80% of the total characters. This trend reflects the Elizabethan times and the dramatic conventions. He wrote his plays in late 16ths and early 17th centuries. A period when gender roles were defines and enforced. Men were dominant, and women were subservient. And yet, Shakespeare subverted, sometimes.

Shakespeare's women were conventional in histories. In tragedies, a little less. In comedies though, they were adventurous. They not only spoke more but with spiritedness. For instance, in "As You Like It", women speak for 40% of the play's duration, and Rosalind dominates much of the action. It is not surprising then, that among the top ten plays where women speak more, eight are comedies. [I am not getting into the problem of problem plays]. Among the other two, one is a history and the other a tragedy. "Henry VI, Part II" features there because Margaret of Anjou plays a pivotal role in shaping the plot. And "Romeo and Juliet" is one of the few plays where a woman is an eponymous character.
